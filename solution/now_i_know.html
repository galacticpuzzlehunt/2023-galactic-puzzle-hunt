Solution: Now I Know
Answer: REPETITION

Written by Ben Yang

The primary aha of this puzzle is that it is based on the fact that “Twinkle, Twinkle, Little Star” and “Baa, Baa, Black Sheep” share the same melody as “The ABC Song”. Solving some of the less vague clues, we notice that the answers are recognizable as parts of the lyrics. In addition, the puzzle’s title references the last line of “The ABC Song” (the common American version): "Now I know my ABCs, next time won’t you sing with me".

Since these share the same melody, we can construct a correspondence between words/phrases/syllables of the rhymes and the letters in the alphabet (plus the word "and").

Author’s Notes

This idea originates from one time I was doing karaoke with friends, and one of them sang alternate lyrics (the Weird Al version) to a song. It was joked that they just enjoyed being contrarian, with another person remarking “I bet you sang ‘Twinkle, Twinkle, Little Star’ instead of the ABCs in elementary school.” My puzzle-obsessed mind thought “Hey, that gives an encoding of the alphabet”, and filed the concept away for later use.

Due to the rhythm of the LMNOP segment of the alphabet, LM or NO needed to be clued as bigrams, rather than as separate letters. This was the reason that the cluephrase included the answer backwards, though I hope solvers were amused by including this eccentricity of the encoding into the puzzle.

Unfortunately, we didn’t figure out a good way to resolve the ambiguity of the words to “Baa, Baa, Black Sheep”. Neither “one for my master, one for my dame” nor “one for the master, one for the dame” seem to be canonical (though the song itself can be canonical in the round sense), and sources are split on the two variations. In the end, we chose “for my” because “for the” had some extra confusion with the (nth of m) disambiguation. There are three instances of “for the” in that version of the lyrics, but the third does not correspond to a full letter (“one for the” matches W). This meant that both (1st of 2) and (1st of 3) felt somewhat misleading or insufficiently correct.
